Slide 02

Why This Project Matters — The Problem Statement

In real competitive games — whether sports, esports, or board games — scoring is never linear. Teams go through phases of dominance, defensive tightening, momentum shifts, and late-game pressure. A simple formula like "Team A scores 2 points per minute" cannot capture these dynamics.

This project addresses a fundamental question: Can we build a mathematical model that captures the interplay between offense, defense, momentum, and competitive pressure, and then numerically solve it to predict plausible score trajectories?

The answer is yes — by formulating score evolution as a system of coupled ODEs and applying well-known numerical solvers, we can generate realistic trajectories and simultaneously study how the choice of solver affects the simulation outcome.

Key arguments to make

  • Scores evolve continuously, not in discrete jumps — this justifies using ODEs instead of simple arithmetic.
  • The two teams are coupled — Team A's performance depends on Team B's defense, and vice versa. This creates feedback loops.
  • Numerical methods are needed because the ODE system is nonlinear — there is no closed-form analytical solution.
  • Comparing multiple solvers on the same model reveals real differences in accuracy, speed, and stability.

Say this: "The problem I'm solving is that real game scoring is dynamic and interactive. A static formula cannot capture how teams influence each other. By treating scores as time-dependent variables governed by differential equations, I can model realistic game behavior and compare numerical methods in a practical context."

Slide 04

The ODE Model — Mathematical Foundation

The simulator tracks the score state of both teams over time as a coupled system:

State Vector 
y(t) = [S_A(t), S_B(t)]    where S_A = Team A score, S_B = Team B score
Full ODE System 
dS_A/dt = max(0, drive_A × pressure_A + clutch_A(t) − defensive_drag_A)
dS_B/dt = max(0, drive_B × pressure_B + clutch_B(t) − defensive_drag_B)

What each term means

  • Drive — The base scoring potential: attack × momentum × tempo × saturation. This is the engine that pushes the score upward.
  • Pressure — A multiplier that changes based on the current lead. The trailing team gets boosted; the leading team gets slightly suppressed. This models psychological pressure.
  • Clutch — A sinusoidal time-varying burst term: clutch × sin(0.9t). This creates periodic scoring runs and momentum swings, mimicking phases in a real game.
  • Defensive Drag — A damping force: opponent_defense × coupling × current_score. As scores grow, defensive resistance increases, preventing unrealistic runaway scoring.

Why this model is coupled

Each team's derivative depends on both teams' scores:

  • Pressure depends on the score difference (L = S_A − S_B)
  • Defensive drag on Team A uses Team B's defense coefficient
  • This coupling means you cannot solve one team's equation independently — they must be solved simultaneously

The max(0, ...) clamp ensures scoring rates never become negative. In a game, scores don't decrease, so this is a physical constraint.

Say this: "The model is a coupled nonlinear ODE system. Each team's scoring rate depends on its own attack and momentum, the score difference through pressure, a periodic clutch term, and defensive damping from the opponent. The coupling means both equations must be solved together, which is why numerical methods are essential."

Slide 05

Model Deep-Dive — Every Component Explained

Saturation Drive

drive_i = attack_i × momentum_i × tempo × max(0.05, 1 − S_i/cap)

As a team's score approaches the score cap, the saturation factor (1 − S/cap) decreases toward zero, naturally slowing scoring growth. The floor at 0.05 prevents complete stagnation — teams always retain minimal scoring ability.

Real-world analogy: In basketball, a team that's already scored a lot may ease off or face tighter rotational defense, reducing their scoring rate naturally.

Competitive Pressure

L = S_A − S_B    (lead difference)
P_A = 1 − pressure_strength × L
P_B = 1 + pressure_strength × L

When Team A leads (L > 0), their pressure factor P_A decreases while Team B's P_B increases. This models the "chaser effect" where trailing teams intensify their play.

Real-world analogy: In football, the team that's behind often pushes harder in final minutes, while the leading team may play conservatively.

Clutch Oscillation

C_A(t) = clutch_A × sin(0.9 × t)
C_B(t) = clutch_B × sin(0.9 × t + 1.1)

The sinusoidal term creates periodic scoring bursts. The phase offset (1.1 radians) ensures teams don't surge simultaneously — when one team is in a scoring run, the other may be in a lull.

Why sine? Sine functions are smooth, periodic, and bounded. They naturally model cyclical momentum shifts without introducing discontinuities.

Defensive Drag

R_A = defense_B × coupling × max(S_A, 0)
R_B = defense_A × coupling × max(S_B, 0)

This is a score-proportional damping force. As a team accumulates more points, the opposing defense exerts greater resistance. This prevents unrealistic exponential score growth.

Why proportional to score? Higher-scoring teams become more predictable targets. The opposing team adapts defensively as the score grows.

Say this: "Each component of the ODE has a clear physical interpretation. The drive term is the engine, pressure adjusts for competitive context, clutch adds periodic momentum swings, and defensive drag prevents unrealistic growth. Together they create a realistic, bounded, coupled system that produces plausible game trajectories."

Slide 06

Variables and Parameters — Complete Reference

🎯 Team Parameters

  • Attack — Offensive drive coefficient. Higher values mean faster scoring potential.
  • Defense — Defensive suppression applied to the opponent's drag term. Higher defense = stronger scoring resistance on the other team.
  • Momentum — Current rhythm/form multiplier. Scales the drive continuously over time.
  • Clutch — Amplitude of the periodic burst term. Higher clutch = stronger scoring runs and deeper lulls.

🌐 Global Parameters

  • Tempo — Match speed multiplier affecting both teams equally.
  • Pressure Strength — How strongly the lead difference influences scoring. Higher = more rubber-banding.
  • Coupling — Cross-team defensive interaction factor. Higher = more suppression for both teams.
  • Score Cap — Saturation threshold. Controls when scoring naturally begins to slow down.

⚙️ Simulation Controls

  • Step Size (h) — Time increment per solver step. Smaller h = higher accuracy but more computation.
  • Steps — Total integration steps. Total simulated time ≈ h × steps.
  • Benchmark Refinement — Factor for the reference RK4. Reference uses h/refinement step size.
  • Initial Scores — Starting points for each team. Non-zero values model comeback scenarios.

Say this: "These parameters let me simulate any game style without changing the mathematical framework. I just adjust coefficients and the same ODE model produces completely different trajectories — from defensive grinds to high-scoring shootouts."

Worked Example — Manual Computation Step

Let's trace one step of Euler vs RK4 to see why they differ. Assume at time t=0, both scores start at 0 with the Balanced Classic scenario:

Euler Method — One Step

# Initial state t = 0, S_A = 0, S_B = 0, h = 0.1 # Compute derivative at (t=0, S_A=0, S_B=0) lead = 0 − 0 = 0 pressure_A = 1 − 0.03 × 0 = 1.0 drive_A = 1.4 × 1.0 × 1.0 × max(0.05, 1 − 0/120) = 1.4 clutch_A = 0.2 × sin(0) = 0 drag_A = 1.0 × 0.02 × 0 = 0 dS_A/dt = max(0, 1.4 × 1.0 + 0 − 0) = 1.4 # Euler update S_A(0.1) = 0 + 0.1 × 1.4 = 0.14

RK4 Method — One Step (Simplified)

# k1: slope at start (same as Euler) k1_A = 1.4 # k2: slope at midpoint using k1 S_A_mid = 0 + 0.05 × 1.4 = 0.07 k2_A = f(0.05, 0.07, ...) ≈ 1.397 # k3: slope at midpoint using k2 S_A_mid2 = 0 + 0.05 × 1.397 = 0.0699 k3_A ≈ 1.397 # k4: slope at end using k3 S_A_end = 0 + 0.1 × 1.397 = 0.1397 k4_A ≈ 1.394 # Weighted combination: (k1 + 2k2 + 2k3 + k4)/6 S_A(0.1) = 0 + (0.1/6)(1.4 + 2(1.397) + 2(1.397) + 1.394) ≈ 0.1398
📊
What this shows

After just one step, the difference is small (0.14 vs 0.1398). But over 240 steps, these tiny per-step errors compound. By the end of the simulation, Euler may drift significantly from the true trajectory while RK4 stays much closer. This is why trajectory RMSE (measuring cumulative path difference) is more meaningful than just comparing final scores.

Say this: "This worked example shows that each method approximates the same derivative differently. Euler takes one slope, RK4 takes four and combines them. The difference per step is tiny, but over hundreds of steps, error accumulates. My project measures this accumulation using trajectory RMSE."

Slide 09

Error Analysis Theory — Why Orders Matter

Every numerical method introduces truncation error — the difference between the true solution and the numerical approximation. Methods are classified by their order of accuracy:

Local Truncation Error (per step) 
Euler:     LTE = O(h²)  → Global error = O(h)
Heun/RK2:  LTE = O(h³)  → Global error = O(h²)
RK4:       LTE = O(h⁵)  → Global error = O(h⁴)

The O(·) notation means: if you halve the step size h, a first-order method halves its error, but a fourth-order method reduces error by a factor of 16 (2⁴). This is the fundamental reason higher-order methods are dramatically more accurate.

Global Error Accumulation 
Global error ≈ N × LTE = (T/h) × O(h^{p+1}) = O(h^p)

where: N = number of steps = T/h
       p = method order
       T = total simulation time

What "order" means in plain language

  • 1st order (Euler): If you make h ten times smaller, error becomes ten times smaller. Linear improvement.
  • 2nd order (Heun/Midpoint/Ralston): If you make h ten times smaller, error becomes 100 times smaller. Quadratic improvement.
  • 4th order (RK4): If you make h ten times smaller, error becomes 10,000 times smaller. This is why RK4 is the reference baseline in this project.

Adams-Bashforth 2 (special case)

AB2 is a 2nd-order multistep method. It achieves 2nd-order accuracy with only one new function evaluation per step (it reuses the previous derivative). This makes it computationally cheaper than single-step RK2 methods but requires a startup step from another method.

Say this: "Order tells us how fast error shrinks when we reduce step size. Euler is first-order, Heun and Midpoint are second-order, and RK4 is fourth-order. My project demonstrates this experimentally: when you look at the benchmark RMSE values, you can see that RK4's error is orders of magnitude smaller than Euler's."

How to Read and Interpret the Results

📊 Score Evolution Chart

  • Each method produces its own pair of lines (Team A solid, Team B dashed)
  • Methods with the same color family share the same solver
  • Lines that overlap closely indicate solver agreement
  • Lines that diverge reveal methods struggling with the nonlinearity
  • Look for: where lines separate (usually at high curvature regions or clutch peaks)

📉 Benchmark Chart

  • Bars show runtime (milliseconds) — taller = slower
  • Orange line shows trajectory RMSE — higher = less accurate
  • The ideal method is in the bottom-left: low bar + low RMSE
  • This dual-axis chart is the most important visual for the accuracy-vs-speed argument

📋 Analytics Table

  • Winner: Which team has the higher final score (may differ by method!)
  • Lead Changes: How many times the lead flipped during simulation
  • Volatility Index: Relative fluctuation — higher values mean a more unpredictable game
  • Comeback Index: How much the winner trailed before winning — higher = more dramatic comeback

🏆 What benchmark tables prove

  • Which method is fastest in raw computation
  • Which method is closest to the reference trajectory (lowest RMSE)
  • Which method offers the best efficiency — the optimal speed-accuracy balance
  • Whether the winner determination is consistent across all methods (if not, solver choice matters for the outcome itself!)

Say this: "The results show that solver choice can even change the predicted winner in tight matches. This is the most powerful argument for why numerical method selection matters — it's not just about speed, it affects the actual outcome."

Slide 15

Limitations and Future Improvements

Current Limitations

  • Deterministic model — No randomness. Real games have stochastic elements (turnovers, lucky shots).
  • Fixed parameters over time — In reality, team dynamics change (substitutions, fatigue, tactical adjustments).
  • Simple oscillation — The clutch term uses a single-frequency sine wave. Real momentum might have irregular patterns.
  • No adaptive stepping — All methods use a fixed step size. Adaptive methods (RK45) would adjust h based on local error.
  • 2D state — Only two score variables. Real models might track individual player contributions.

Potential Future Work

  • Stochastic differential equations (SDEs) — Add Brownian noise terms for randomness.
  • Adaptive step-size control — Implement Runge-Kutta-Fehlberg (RKF45) with error estimation.
  • Time-varying parameters — Let attack/defense change based on fatigue curves.
  • Multi-team tournaments — Extend to track multiple matches in a tournament bracket.
  • Machine learning integration — Train parameters from real game data to calibrate the model.
  • Implicit methods — Implement backward Euler or trapezoid rule for stiff system comparison.

Say this: "Every model has limitations. Mine is deterministic and uses fixed parameters. But these limitations are conscious design choices that keep the project focused on numerical method comparison. Future work could add randomness, adaptive stepping, and real data calibration."

Slide 16

Closing Statement & Answering Questions

This project demonstrates that mathematical modeling through coupled ODEs can simulate realistic competitive score evolution. By implementing and benchmarking six numerical solvers, I've shown that solver choice is not trivial — it affects trajectory accuracy, computational cost, and even the predicted match outcome. The project bridges theory and practice: every formula has a clear physical interpretation, every method has measurable differences, and the interactive simulator makes the mathematics visible and understandable.

Q: Why not just use a spreadsheet formula? Because game scoring involves feedback loops, pressure effects, and momentum — these require differential equations, not static formulas.
Q: Why compare so many methods? To quantitatively demonstrate the accuracy-cost trade-off that is central to numerical analysis. One method alone doesn't teach the comparison lesson.
Q: Is RK4 always the best? It's the most accurate, but not always the best choice. If speed matters more than precision, a simpler method with smaller h could be more efficient overall.
Q: How do you know your reference solution is correct? The reference uses RK4 with {{ inputs.benchmark_refinement }}× finer step size. While not the analytical solution, it's orders of magnitude more accurate than any coarse method.
Q: What makes this different from a random number generator? Everything in this simulator is deterministic and mathematically governed. Given the same parameters, it always produces the same output. The trajectories are physically meaningful, not random.
Q: Could this model be used for real game prediction? With parameter calibration from historical data, yes. The model structure is flexible enough; it would need statistical fitting to real team performance metrics.

Final line: "This project is a practical bridge between numerical methods theory and real-world game analytics. It shows that mathematics is not just abstract — it can simulate, predict, and explain dynamic systems in a way that is both rigorous and visually compelling."
